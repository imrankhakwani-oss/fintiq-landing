Building Your Optimal Portfolio with the Efficient Frontier — Step by Step

Updated July 2026 · 18 min read · Advanced

Markowitz's 1952 paper showed that portfolio risk is not the weighted average of individual stock risks — it depends critically on how stocks move relative to each other. Two stocks that are individually risky can, when combined, produce a portfolio that is less risky than either alone. This is the mathematical foundation of diversification, and the Efficient Frontier is its visual expression.

Step 3: Understanding the Output

Fintiq plots three key points on the Efficient Frontier:

  • Minimum Variance Portfolio (MVP): The leftmost point — lowest possible portfolio volatility. Often has a lower expected return but maximum diversification benefit.
  • Maximum Sharpe Ratio Portfolio (Tangency Portfolio): The point where the Capital Market Line is tangent to the frontier. This is the mathematically optimal risk-adjusted portfolio — maximum return per unit of risk.
  • Maximum Return Portfolio: The rightmost efficient point — highest expected return but highest volatility. Usually 90%+ concentrated in 1–2 stocks.

Step 5: Rebalancing Rules

The optimal weights change as correlations, returns, and volatilities shift over time. Professional practice:

  • Quarterly review: Re-run the optimisation in Fintiq every quarter. If any weight has drifted more than 5 percentage points from the target, rebalance.
  • Event-triggered review: Rebalance after major macro shifts (central bank policy changes, geopolitical events) that structurally change correlations.
  • Tax efficiency: In an ISA, rebalancing is free of CGT. In a GIA, consider tax-loss harvesting — selling underperformers to offset gains in winners before rebalancing.

Step 6: Constraints and Real-World Adjustments

Pure mathematical optimisation can produce impractical results — e.g. 0% in most stocks and 40% in one. Apply these constraints in Fintiq's settings:

  • Minimum weight per stock: 3% (ensures genuine diversification)
  • Maximum weight per stock: 25% (limits concentration risk)
  • Maximum sector weight: 30% (prevents sector concentration)
Key Insight: Correlation Is Everything
Adding a stock with a lower expected return than your existing holdings can improve your portfolio's Sharpe ratio if it has a sufficiently low or negative correlation. This is why National Grid (modest returns, very low volatility) earns a 14% weighting alongside high-growth AstraZeneca — the combination is mathematically superior to holding either alone.
