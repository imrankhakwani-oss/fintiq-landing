Complete FTSE 100 Stock Screening Masterclass

Updated July 2026 · 14 min read · Intermediate to Advanced

Most retail investors approach the FTSE 100 the same way: they look for stocks that have fallen, check the dividend yield, and buy those with the most familiar names. It is an approach that feels prudent — buying well-known companies that have come down in price — but research consistently shows it underperforms systematically constructed, multi-factor screens by a significant margin.

Professional fund managers do not browse for ideas. They systematically screen the universe of available stocks against multiple criteria simultaneously, eliminating the vast majority of candidates before spending analytical time on the survivors. This guide teaches you that approach: how to screen the FTSE 100 like a professional, using multiple factors in combination rather than in isolation.

Why Single Metrics Fail

The single-metric screening trap is well-documented in academic and practitioner literature. Each popular single-metric screen has a fatal flaw:

  • Lowest P/E only: Catches "value traps" — businesses that are cheap because earnings are about to collapse.
  • Highest dividend yield only: A 9% dividend yield is often a warning sign. The market has priced in a dividend cut.
  • Lowest price-to-book only: Works badly for modern businesses where intangible assets dominate balance sheets.
  • Highest ROE only: ROE can be inflated by high leverage. A bank can achieve 20% ROE with 15x leverage — not the same quality as 20% ROE with minimal debt.

The Three Pillars of Professional Screening

Pillar 1: Quality — Pillar 2: Value — Pillar 3: Momentum

The highest-probability opportunities arise when all three pillars align simultaneously. Quality ensures the underlying business is strong. Value ensures you are not overpaying. Momentum confirms the fundamental trend is improving rather than deteriorating.

The Exact Multi-Factor Screen Setup in Fintiq

Open Fintiq's stock screener and set the exchange to FTSE 100. Apply filters in this order:

  1. Quality gate: ROE > 12% AND Operating Margin > sector average AND Debt/Equity < 1.5
  2. Value gate: P/E below 5-year average for that company OR EV/EBITDA < 12 for industrials / < 10 for financials
  3. Momentum gate: RSI between 45–65 (positive but not overbought) AND 12-month price momentum in top 40% of sector
  4. FCF confirmation: Free Cash Flow Yield > 4% (Operating Cash Flow minus CapEx, divided by Market Cap)

This combination typically reduces the FTSE 100 to 8–15 candidates — a manageable number for deeper fundamental analysis.

The Post-Results Opportunity Window

One of the most reliable opportunities in FTSE 100 screening occurs in the 5 trading days after a results announcement where the market over-reacts to short-term guidance. The pattern:

  • Company reports in-line or slightly below short-term consensus but reaffirms medium-term targets
  • Stock falls 5–12% on the day of results
  • Your quality screen still passes (the business hasn't deteriorated)
  • The value metric now looks more attractive post-fall
  • RSI drops into the 35–45 range (oversold but not in freefall)

In Fintiq, run your screen immediately after market close on results days. Flag any quality companies that have fallen significantly — these are your highest-priority candidates for deeper analysis.

The Full Process — Summary
1. Determine macro regime → filter to favoured sectors in Fintiq
2. Apply quality gate (ROE, margin, debt using sector-specific thresholds above)
3. Apply value gate (use sector-appropriate metric)
4. Apply momentum gate (RSI 45–65, positive 12M momentum)
5. Confirm FCF yield > 4%
6. Deep-dive the 8–15 survivors: read last 2 annual reports, understand the business model, check competitive positioning
7. Size position based on conviction and portfolio construction rules

When to Exit

The screen that gets you in should also trigger your exit review. Re-run your screen on existing holdings quarterly. Exit signals:

  • Quality deteriorates: ROE falls below threshold for two consecutive quarters
  • Valuation becomes expensive: P/E or EV/EBITDA exceeds the 5-year high
  • Momentum turns negative: RSI drops below 40 on falling volume without a clear macro reason
  • The original thesis is broken: the catalyst you identified has played out or been negated by new information
