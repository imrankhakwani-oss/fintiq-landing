Pairs Trading with Cointegration: A Practical Guide

Updated July 2026 · 22 min read · Advanced

Pairs trading is a market-neutral strategy that profits from the temporary divergence of two historically correlated securities. When two stocks that normally move together suddenly diverge, you simultaneously buy the underperformer and short the outperformer — betting on their eventual convergence. Because you are simultaneously long and short, your P&L is theoretically independent of overall market direction.

Step 2: Run the Cointegration Test in Fintiq

  1. Open Fintiq → Pairs Trading tab
  2. Enter both ticker symbols (e.g. SHEL and BP.)
  3. Set lookback: 2 years of daily data (520 trading days)
  4. Fintiq runs the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test on the spread
  5. A p-value below 0.05 confirms cointegration — the pair is statistically valid
  6. Review the half-life: ideally 10–30 trading days (2–6 weeks). Too short = noise. Too long = capital tied up too long.

Step 3: Z-Score Entry and Exit Rules

Z-Score = (Current Spread - Mean Spread) / Standard Deviation of Spread Entry rules: Long the underperformer, Short the outperformer when |Z| > 2.0 Exit rules: Close the trade when Z-score reverts to 0 (mean reversion complete) Stop-loss: close if |Z| > 3.5 (spread has widened further, thesis breaking down) Position sizing: Dollar-neutral: equal £ value long and short Beta-neutral: adjust position sizes by each stock's beta to neutralise market exposure

Step 4: Position Sizing and Risk Management

The most common mistake in pairs trading is using equal share counts rather than equal values. Always size positions to be pound-neutral (or beta-neutral for more sophisticated management):

  • Pound-neutral example: If you're pairing Shell at 2,800p vs BP at 500p, and you allocate £5,000 long to Shell (178 shares), you need £5,000 short in BP (1,000 shares). Equal cash value, not equal shares.
  • Beta-neutral: If Shell has beta 0.9 and BP has beta 1.1, adjust: Shell position × 0.9 = BP position × 1.1. This removes the market factor more completely.
  • Maximum pair allocation: No single pair should exceed 10% of total portfolio. Pairs trading works at the portfolio level — run 5–10 pairs simultaneously for the strategy to perform consistently.

When Pairs Break Down — And Why
Cointegration can break permanently due to structural changes: a merger announcement (one stock gets a takeover premium permanently), a major regulatory change affecting one but not the other (e.g. ring-fencing legislation for UK banks), or a commodity market structural shift (e.g. different net-zero transition exposure for Shell vs BP). Always check news before entering a pair that has moved beyond 2.5 standard deviations — the divergence may be news-driven and permanent rather than noise-driven and temporary.
