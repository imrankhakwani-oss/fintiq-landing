Complete FTSE 100 Stock Screening Masterclass

Updated July 2026 · 14 min read · Intermediate to Advanced

Most retail investors approach the FTSE 100 the same way: they look for stocks that have fallen, check the dividend yield, and buy those with the most familiar names. It is an approach that feels prudent — buying well-known companies that have come down in price — but research consistently shows it underperforms systematically constructed, multi-factor screens by a significant margin.

Professional fund managers do not browse for ideas. They systematically screen the universe of available stocks against multiple criteria simultaneously, eliminating the vast majority of candidates before spending analytical time on the survivors. This guide teaches you that approach: how to screen the FTSE 100 like a professional, using multiple factors in combination rather than in isolation.

Why Single Metrics Fail

The single-metric screening trap is well-documented in academic and practitioner literature. Each popular single-metric screen has a fatal flaw:

  • Lowest P/E only: Catches "value traps" — businesses that are cheap because earnings are about to collapse. A housebuilder on 6x P/E before a housing downturn looks cheap; six months later earnings have halved and the P/E was not a bargain.
  • Highest dividend yield only: A 9% dividend yield is often a warning sign, not an opportunity. The market has priced in a dividend cut. Many of the FTSE's highest-yielding stocks subsequently cut their dividends within 18 months.
  • Lowest price-to-book only: Works badly for modern businesses where intangible assets (brands, software, intellectual property) dominate balance sheets. A pharmaceutical company's patents do not appear on the balance sheet at fair value.
  • Highest ROE only: ROE can be inflated by high leverage, which also increases risk. A bank can achieve 20% ROE with 15x leverage; that is not the same quality of return as 20% ROE with minimal debt.

The solution is to combine multiple factors that measure different aspects of business quality simultaneously. When a company screens well on all pillars, the confidence in the investment case increases substantially.

The Three Pillars of Professional Screening

Pillar 1: Quality

Quality metrics measure the structural health and competitive advantage of the business. Key quality indicators include:

  • Return on Equity (ROE): Net profit as a percentage of shareholders' equity. Above 15% suggests strong profitability relative to the capital employed.
  • Operating Margin: Measures pricing power and operational efficiency. Compare to sector average — a margin significantly above the sector average suggests a genuine competitive advantage.
  • Free Cash Flow Conversion: What percentage of operating profit converts to actual cash? High conversion (above 80%) indicates quality earnings; low conversion may suggest accounting practices inflating reported profits.
  • Debt/Equity Ratio: Financial leverage. Below 1.0 is broadly conservative; above 2.0 warrants careful analysis of debt terms and cash flow coverage.
  • Interest Coverage: Operating profit divided by interest expense. Above 5x is healthy; below 2x is a potential stress indicator.

Pillar 2: Value

Value metrics measure whether you are paying a reasonable price for the quality you are buying. The classic value trap is buying a low-quality business cheaply; the better opportunity is buying a high-quality business at a fair or reasonable price.

  • P/E Ratio: Price divided by earnings per share. Always compare against the company's own historical P/E range and the sector average, not an absolute threshold.
  • EV/EBITDA: Enterprise Value divided by earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation. More comparable across companies with different capital structures than P/E. Below 10x is broadly attractive for most FTSE 100 sectors.
  • Price/Free Cash Flow: More conservative than P/E because it uses actual cash generation rather than accounting earnings. Below 15x is generally attractive.
  • Price/Book: Useful for financials (banks, insurance) but less so for IP-heavy businesses. Below 1.5x is attractive for most sectors; banks below 1.0x price-to-book are often statistically cheap.

Pillar 3: Momentum

Academic research consistently shows that stocks with positive price momentum continue to outperform in the medium term (3-12 months). Combining momentum with quality and value significantly improves the signal-to-noise ratio.

  • 12-month price momentum: Stocks in the top 30% of 12-month price performance within their sector tend to continue outperforming. This reflects genuine fundamental momentum (improving earnings, analyst upgrades) rather than pure speculation.
  • Earnings revision momentum: When analysts revise earnings estimates upward, it typically precedes further upgrades. Stocks with consistent positive estimate revisions are in a fundamentally improving trend.
  • RSI (Relative Strength Index): A technical momentum indicator. RSI in the 50-70 range suggests positive but not overbought momentum — the sweet spot for momentum-driven entry.

Why Each Sector Needs Different Screening Criteria

One of the most common mistakes retail investors make is applying identical screening thresholds across all sectors. The metrics that indicate quality in a pharmaceutical company are fundamentally different from those that indicate quality in a bank or a retailer.

Banks: ROE and Price/Book are the primary metrics. Banks use leverage to generate returns, so their operating margins are not comparable to industrial companies. A bank with 12-15% ROE and 0.8x Price/Book is cheap; the same numbers mean something entirely different for a manufacturer.

Mining companies: Commodity companies' earnings are highly cyclical. Screening for low P/E at the peak of a commodity cycle will give you a misleading signal — those earnings are about to normalise downward. Professional analysts use "mid-cycle" or "normalised" earnings estimates rather than trailing reported earnings.

Pharmaceutical companies: High P/E ratios reflect long-duration earnings streams from patented drugs. A pharma on 20x P/E with a rich pipeline, high margins, and strong FCF conversion may be far better value than a retailer on 10x P/E with thin margins and no pricing power.

The Quality-Value Sweet Spot

The highest-probability investment opportunities in the FTSE 100 consistently come from companies that are simultaneously high-quality and reasonably valued. The combination is rare because the market generally recognises quality and prices it accordingly. But it occurs during:

  • Sector rotation periods when capital flows away from a sector temporarily, depressing valuations of good companies alongside bad ones
  • Company-specific controversies that are temporary (a profit warning from a one-off cost, a management change) rather than structural
  • Market-wide selloffs that hit all stocks indiscriminately, including those with no fundamental deterioration
  • Post-results days when the market over-reacts to short-term guidance disappointments in companies with strong long-term fundamentals

Finding these opportunities requires a systematic screen that flags quality companies (Pillar 1) trading at below-average valuations (Pillar 2) with no deteriorating momentum signal that would suggest the cheapness is justified (Pillar 3).
