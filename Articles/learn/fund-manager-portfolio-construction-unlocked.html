How Professional Fund Managers Construct Portfolios — and How to Copy Them

Updated July 2026 · 22 min read · Advanced

Professional fund managers follow a rigorous top-down process before a single stock is selected. The stock is the last decision, not the first. Understanding this process — and replicating it with Fintiq — is what separates investors who consistently outperform from those who chase tips.

Stage 1: Macro Framework — Where Are We in the Cycle?

Every portfolio construction decision flows from the macro regime. Professional managers start every quarter with a macro assessment across five dimensions:

DimensionCurrent Assessment (update quarterly)Portfolio Implication
Growth trajectoryAccelerating / Decelerating / StableCyclicals vs Defensives
Inflation directionRising / Falling / StableReal assets vs Bonds
Central bank stanceTightening / Easing / NeutralDuration exposure
Credit conditionsLoose / Tight / TighteningLeverage tolerance
Geopolitical riskElevated / Normal / DecliningSafe haven allocation

Stage 2: Asset Allocation — The Most Important Decision

Academic research consistently shows that over 90% of long-term portfolio returns are explained by asset allocation, not stock selection. Institutional target allocations by macro regime:

RegimeGlobal EquitiesUK EquitiesBondsCommodities/Real AssetsCash
Growth + Low Inflation (goldilocks)45%15%25%10%5%
Growth + High Inflation35%15%10%35%5%
Recession + Low Inflation20%10%45%10%15%
Stagflation15%10%5%55%15%

Stage 3: Sector Allocation — Overweight and Underweight Decisions

Within the equity allocation, fund managers take active positions relative to the index. A UK equity fund with 40% financials in the benchmark might go to 50% (overweight) or 30% (underweight) based on their macro view. The process:

  1. Start with the benchmark sector weights (FTSE 100 composition)
  2. Apply the macro overlay — which sectors benefit from your growth/inflation/rate view?
  3. Run a sector-level quality screen — avoid sectors with deteriorating fundamentals regardless of macro
  4. Set sector bounds: maximum overweight of +10%, maximum underweight of -8% (prevents concentrated sector bets)

Stage 4: Stock Selection Within Sectors

With sector allocations set, stock selection becomes a relative decision within each sector rather than an absolute one. The fund manager is choosing the best stocks within a sector, not necessarily the best stocks in the market. This is why sector-specific quality metrics matter so much — see the FTSE 100 Masterclass guide for sector thresholds.

Stage 5: Position Sizing — The Kelly Criterion in Practice

Professional managers size positions based on conviction and diversification benefit, not equal weighting. The simplified Kelly approach:

Kelly position size = (Edge × Odds) / Variance Simplified practical rule for retail investors: High conviction (3+ quality signals, clear catalyst) = 5–8% position Medium conviction (2 quality signals, reasonable valuation) = 2–4% Speculative (1 signal, higher uncertainty) = 1–2% Maximum single position: 8% (prevents any single stock destroying the portfolio) Minimum meaningful position: 1% (below this, the position doesn't move the needle) For a £50,000 portfolio: High conviction: £2,500–£4,000 per position (6–10 positions) Diversified: £1,000–£2,000 per position (25–50 positions)

Stage 6: Risk Budget Management

Professional portfolio managers track portfolio-level risk, not just individual position risk. Key metrics to monitor monthly in Fintiq:

  • Portfolio beta: How sensitive is the portfolio to market moves? Beta > 1.2 means the portfolio will amplify market swings — acceptable in bull markets, dangerous in downturns.
  • Sector concentration: No sector should exceed 30% (FTSE 100 itself has 20% financials — be aware of this when holding UK equity funds alongside individual stocks).
  • Correlation clustering: Check that your holdings are not all moving together. Run the portfolio through Fintiq's correlation matrix — if most correlations are above 0.7, you have less diversification than you think.
  • Geographic concentration: FTSE 100 stocks have ~75% of revenues from outside the UK — but they are priced in GBP, creating currency risk. Intentional currency diversification through genuinely international holdings is different from inadvertent GBP concentration.

The Monthly Portfolio Review Process

  1. Update macro assessment — has the regime changed? Adjust asset allocation if needed.
  2. Re-run Fintiq screen on existing holdings — any that no longer pass your quality/value/momentum criteria?
  3. Check portfolio metrics — beta, sector weights, correlation matrix.
  4. Review position sizes — any that have drifted significantly from target? Trim winners that have become outsized; consider adding to laggards if thesis intact.
  5. Generate new candidate list — run your full screen to see what new opportunities have emerged.
  6. Document your decisions — use Fintiq's journal. Writing down your reasoning at the time is invaluable when reviewing performance later.
The Fund Manager Mindset
Professional fund managers think in terms of relative performance and marginal contribution to risk and return. Every position is justified by answering: "What does this holding add to my portfolio that I don't already have?" A sixth UK bank holding adds almost nothing — you already have the UK banking factor. A position in a healthcare stock with negative correlation to your financial holdings adds genuine diversification value even if its standalone return outlook is modest.
