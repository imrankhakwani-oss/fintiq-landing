What is Monte Carlo Simulation? A Simple Guide for Investors

⏱ 7 min read · Last updated July 2026 · Try the Retirement Planner

The Origin Story: From Casino to Laboratory to Your Portfolio

The story of Monte Carlo simulation begins not with finance, but with physics — and with a card game played during a period of illness.

It was 1946. Stanislaw Ulam, a brilliant Polish-American mathematician working at Los Alamos National Laboratory in New Mexico, was recovering from encephalitis. During his convalescence, he played a great deal of solitaire (patience), the solo card game. As his mathematical mind worked through the problem, he found himself asking a question that had no simple analytical answer: what is the probability of winning a game of solitaire if all the cards are shuffled randomly?

The theoretical calculation — accounting for every possible arrangement of 52 cards and every possible sequence of moves — would have been practically impossible to compute directly. But Ulam realised there was a much simpler approach: instead of computing the probability mathematically, you could simply play thousands of games and count how many you won. The proportion of wins would converge on the true probability.

Ulam shared the idea with his colleague John von Neumann, one of the most extraordinary mathematical minds of the twentieth century. Von Neumann immediately grasped its potential for the far more complex computational problems they were working on at Los Alamos — problems related to neutron diffusion in nuclear weapons research. He helped formalise the method and championed its use.

The name "Monte Carlo" was coined by another Los Alamos mathematician, Nicholas Metropolis, as a code name for the technique — a reference to the Monte Carlo Casino in Monaco, where Ulam's uncle would borrow money to gamble. The connection was apt: both the casino and the method relied fundamentally on the mathematics of randomness and probability.

Today, Monte Carlo simulation is used across physics, engineering, finance, medicine, climate science, and logistics. In investing, it has become one of the most powerful tools available for modelling uncertain futures.

The Core Concept: Modelling Many Futures Instead of One

The fundamental problem with forecasting investments is that the future is uncertain. Markets do not produce the same return every year. They produce wildly varying returns — sometimes extremely positive, sometimes extremely negative — that follow a broad statistical distribution but cannot be predicted for any specific year.

Traditional financial planning tools deal with this uncertainty by pretending it doesn't exist. A compound interest calculator asks: "What is your expected annual return?" and then projects a single, deterministic path forward. If you say 7%, it compounds your money at exactly 7% per year for however many years you choose. The result looks precise and certain.

But it is false precision. Markets have never returned exactly 7% in any given year. The actual return is always different — sometimes wildly so.

Monte Carlo simulation takes the opposite approach. Instead of assuming one future, it models thousands of possible futures simultaneously, each one a different random draw from the statistical distribution of historical returns. Run 10,000 simulations, and you have 10,000 different possible paths your portfolio might take. The result is not a single number, but a distribution of outcomes — a picture of all the ways your investment journey might unfold.

How It Works in Practice

The mechanics of a Monte Carlo simulation for investing are straightforward once you understand the intuition.

Consider a global equity portfolio that has historically returned an average of 7% per year, with a standard deviation (a measure of volatility) of approximately 15%. This means that in any given year, returns typically fall within the range of -8% to +22%, and occasionally fall outside even that range in particularly good or bad years.

The simulation works as follows:

  1. Draw a random annual return from the historical distribution (average 7%, standard deviation 15%). This might produce +12% for Year 1.
  2. Apply that return to your starting portfolio value. A £50,000 portfolio at +12% becomes £56,000 at the end of Year 1.
  3. Draw another random return for Year 2. This might produce -8%. The £56,000 portfolio falls to £51,520.
  4. Repeat for every year of your investment horizon. Thirty years of random returns, one per year. At the end, record the final portfolio value. This is one "simulation" or "path."
  5. Run the entire process 10,000 times. Each simulation produces a different sequence of random annual returns and therefore a different final portfolio value.
  6. Analyse the distribution of 10,000 final values. This distribution — showing the full range of outcomes from worst to best — is the Monte Carlo result.
Simple Monte Carlo Example Starting portfolio: £50,000 Time horizon: 20 years Annual contributions: £0 (lump sum only) Return assumption: Mean 7%, Std Dev 15% Simulations: 10,000 Results after 10,000 simulations: 10th percentile (bad luck): ~£68,000 25th percentile: ~£110,000 50th percentile (median): ~£175,000 75th percentile: ~£270,000 90th percentile (good luck): ~£420,000 Range of outcomes: £68,000 to over £500,000

What the Output Means: Reading Percentile Bands

The most important output from a Monte Carlo simulation is the percentile distribution of outcomes. Here is how to read it:

The 10th percentile outcome means that 90% of simulations produced a better result — this is your downside case. If you are comfortable with the portfolio value at the 10th percentile, your plan is robust against most bad-luck scenarios.

The 50th percentile (median) is the middle outcome — half of simulations ended above this value, half below. It is a more realistic "expected" outcome than a compound interest calculator would give, because it implicitly accounts for the impact of return variability on the median outcome (which is lower than the arithmetic mean due to volatility drag).

The 90th percentile shows the good-luck scenario — the top 10% of outcomes. It is the potential upside if fortune smiles on your investment journey.

Viewing these three bands together gives you an honest picture of the range of possible futures — not a false single-point prediction.

Why This Beats Compound Interest Calculators

If you invest £50,000 and compound it at 7% for 20 years, a standard calculator gives you £193,484 — one single, certain-seeming number. It is the answer to a question that the real world will never actually pose to you, because real markets do not return 7% every year.

The compound interest calculator gives false certainty. The Monte Carlo simulation gives honest uncertainty.

The difference matters enormously in practice, for a reason that is not immediately obvious: the sequence in which returns arrive matters just as much as the average return itself.

Sequence of Returns Risk: The Most Underestimated Risk in Retirement

Sequence of returns risk is the danger that the order of investment returns — not just their average — can dramatically affect your final portfolio value. It is particularly important for investors who are withdrawing from their portfolio (in retirement) rather than adding to it.

Here is a worked example that makes this concrete:

Imagine two investors, both of whom retire with exactly £500,000 and withdraw £25,000 per year (5% of the starting value). Both experience an average return of 4% per year over their 30-year retirement. The only difference: the sequence of those returns.

Investor A retires in a bull market. Her first five years see returns of +18%, +22%, +15%, +12%, +20%. The portfolio grows strongly early, giving her a large cushion. Even when poor returns arrive in later years, her pot is big enough to absorb them. After 30 years, she has approximately £800,000 remaining.

Investor B retires in a bear market. His first five years see returns of -25%, -18%, -12%, -8%, -15%. The portfolio falls sharply just as he begins withdrawing. Each withdrawal takes a larger proportion of a shrinking pot. Even when good returns arrive in later years, the portfolio is too depleted to benefit fully. Investor B runs out of money at year 22 — eight years before he expected to.

Same average return. Same starting amount. Same annual withdrawal. Completely different outcomes.

A compound interest calculator using a fixed 4% return would have shown both investors the same (incorrect) projection. Monte Carlo simulation captures this sequence risk correctly, because each simulation draws its returns in a random order — and the distribution of final outcomes therefore reflects all the possible sequences, not just the average.

For retirees, sequence of returns risk is the single biggest financial planning risk. If a bear market strikes in the early years of retirement, the combination of falling portfolio values and continued withdrawals can permanently impair a portfolio's ability to recover — even if markets subsequently perform well.

The Probability of Success: What You're Really Aiming For

The "probability of success" metric — the percentage of simulations where your financial goal is met — is the most actionable output from a Monte Carlo analysis.

As a general guideline:

  • Above 90%: very robust plan — you could probably afford to save less or spend more
  • 80–90%: strong plan — comfortable level of resilience
  • 70–80%: acceptable — worth monitoring and adjusting if market conditions deteriorate
  • 60–70%: fragile — a bad-luck sequence of returns could cause real problems
  • Below 60%: high risk — the plan needs significant revision (more saving, later retirement, lower target)

Most financial planners target a success probability of 80–90% for retirement planning. This does not mean the plan will definitely succeed — it means it is robust enough to survive the vast majority of historical market scenarios.

The Limitations You Need to Understand

Monte Carlo simulation is powerful, but it has real limitations that informed users should understand:

  • It relies on historical data. The return and volatility parameters used in the simulation are based on historical market data. If the future is structurally different from the past — as it always is to some degree — the simulation may systematically underestimate or overestimate returns.
  • It does not model true black swans. Events like a 30-year depression, a complete breakdown of market infrastructure, or a catastrophic geopolitical event may lie outside the distribution used in the simulation.
  • It assumes returns are independently distributed. In reality, markets exhibit some degree of autocorrelation (bad years can cluster together). Some Monte Carlo models account for this; others do not.
  • The output is a probability, not a guarantee. A 90% success rate means 10% of simulations failed. If you happen to live through one of those scenarios, the simulation gave you no warning of which future you were in.

Using Monte Carlo Alongside Traditional Planning

The best approach is to use Monte Carlo simulation as a stress-testing and planning tool, not as a prediction. Run the simulation, note the success probability, and then ask: "What would I need to change to reach 85%+ success?"

Often the answer is surprisingly small adjustments: saving £200 more per month, working two years longer, targeting a slightly lower retirement income. The simulation makes these trade-offs explicit and quantifiable rather than abstract.
