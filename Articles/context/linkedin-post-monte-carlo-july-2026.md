# LinkedIn Post — Monte Carlo Article Relaunch

---

Your pension calculator is lying to you.

Not maliciously. It just assumes markets return the same amount every year.
They never have. Not once.

In 2022, global equities fell 18%.
In 2019, they rose 27%.
In 2008, they fell 38%.

A calculator that smooths all of that into a tidy "7% per year" line is telling you a story — not a plan.

---

Monte Carlo simulation does something different.

Instead of projecting one future, it runs 5,000 of them simultaneously — each one a different random sequence of market returns calibrated to how markets have actually behaved.

Some of those 5,000 futures are great. Markets cooperate, your pot compounds beautifully, you retire early.

Some are brutal. A bear market arrives in year one of retirement, your withdrawals accelerate the damage, and even a subsequent recovery can't save you.

The spread between those futures is your retirement risk. And no straight-line calculator can see it.

---

The specific danger is called sequence of returns risk.

Two investors. Same starting pot (£500,000). Same annual withdrawal (£25,000). Same average return over 30 years (4%).

The only difference: the order of those returns.

Investor A gets the good years early. At year 30 she has £800,000 remaining.

Investor B gets the bad years first. He runs out of money at year 22.

Same average. Same amount. Completely different endings.

This is why "what's your expected annual return?" is the wrong question to anchor a retirement plan on.
